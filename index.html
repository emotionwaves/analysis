Contagious laughter: Descriptive analysis

 

 

Participants

 

Participants were recruited via ﻿University of Amsterdam, Department of Psychology, and University of California, Department of Communication Studies’ research pools. ﻿Participation in the study was compensated with course credit. In addition, we utilized personal networks, social media, and news media to maximize number of participants.

 

Perceivers

 

A total of 714 participants (503 women, 198 men, 7 prefer not to say, 5 other; ﻿Mage = 28.08, SDage = 12.60, range = 16-76 years old) completed the study. Participants rated contagiousness on a scale from 1 (“not al all”) to 5 (“very much”) after hearing laughter clips (﻿M = 1.90, SD = 1.25). Number of laughter clips was 11.90 on average (SD = 9.01; range =1-57). In general, participants considered the laughing person as from their own culture 59 % and another culture 41 % of the time.

 

Producers

 

A total of 64 producers’ (33 women, 28 men, 3 other; Mage = 31.03, SDage = 13.71, range = 6-75 years old) laughter clips were uploaded to the experiment’ interface. Two of these recordings did not include any downloadable file. Recordings with so much noise (3), speech (2), and laughter of multiple people (3) were not presented to the perceivers. In total, 54 of these recordings were used in the perception experiment.

 

Laughter context: Text data

 

For text analysis, I used R’s tidyverse package. First, tokenization is used to transform the data into a different format, in which individual words are entered as one observation per row. I then filtered the data for stop words (e.g., “the”, “and”, “to”), and merged “laugh”, “laughs”, and “laughing” into “laughter” using stemming. Most common 20 words are shown in Figure 1.

 

Figure 1. Most common 20 words used in the text data describing the context in which contagious laughter occurs.

 

In order to conduct sentiment analysis, I used Bing (Figure 2), Laughran (Figure 3), and Nrc (Figure 4) lexicons. These dictionaries use different categorisations of words.
